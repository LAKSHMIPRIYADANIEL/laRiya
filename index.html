ARTIST'S STATEMENT

I am Lakshmi Priya Daniel, Associate Professor teaching for the past two decades in the Department of Fine Arts, Stella Maris (Autonomous) College in Chennai, Tamil Nadu, India. I am a practicing artist working under the pseudonym laRiya and earlier painted using the acrylic medium primarily on reed mat. My works are based on concepts derived from the Indian cultural ethos and often resonate with a numerical integrity

Currently my artistic journey has taken me into performance art which revolves around experiential narratives and creates installation art from these solo events. The performance artwork which I have chosen to recycle in this exhibition originated with an idea that was equally narcissistic and generic in its resonance. Through my journey of 366 days in the 366 seconds of my performance I reconstruct moments lost in order to fleetingly re-establish a tenuous relationship with the past. The objects which I use to connect with this time are metallic eyes woven into the strands of hair. These mundane artefacts are records of a system of monitoring and surveillance that is often imposed upon women in both an intimate and a pan Indian context.

My connection with Marcia Neblett and the Fulbright program began in the winter semester of 2014 when she was a Fulbright Specialist in Printmaking and a visiting lecturer in Fine Arts from the Savannah College of Art and Design. We have a IV year B.V.A. programme in the Department of Fine Arts in Stella Maris College and in 2014 Marcia conducted several Printmaking and Illustration workshops. We collaborated in the three months that she spent in my institution not only in terms of the teaching workshops but also did a mural painting project for the walls of the college. After her return to the United States we have been in constant touch and I saw with pride her TEDx Speech, “Against the Grain: Teaching Printmaking in India” in 2015.While I was in the United States in June 2018 as part of a leadership training program at Harvard University I got the opportunity to meet Marcia again and we shared our many experiences over the years.

I hold a doctorate from the University of Madras wherein my area of focus was on Gender and art which I approached through the lens of women artists from South India. As a teacher of art my artistic practice often feeds my pedagogical methodologies and this has remained one of the ways in which academician-artists like Marcia Neblett and I have been able to straddle both worlds and remain linked. So in many ways this Fulbright artistic collaboration is the outcome of the strength of collective visions and open dialogues creating bridges and bonds for the future.
